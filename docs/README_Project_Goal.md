# AI Market Thesis Tracker

## Purpose
This project is a structured investing research workspace for tracking market theses, thematic baskets, and prediction accuracy over time.

The goal is not to day trade, automate hype, or defend prior opinions. The goal is to:
- identify durable market ideas
- track the evidence for and against them
- express those ideas through baskets of public equities
- improve decision quality over time
- build long-term wealth with better discipline

## Core Principles
1. Themes first, tickers second.
2. Price action alone is not evidence.
3. Market reaction matters, but only in context.
4. Strong counter-narratives should always be considered.
5. Disconfirming evidence is more valuable than confirming evidence.
6. Not every tracked ticker should be owned.
7. A good story without a clear test is not a usable thesis.
8. This system exists to improve judgment, not automate blind conviction.

## How This Project Will Be Used
This project will be used to:
- define and refine market theses
- organize tickers into themed baskets
- assess earnings and filings against scorecards
- track narrative drift
- review prediction accuracy monthly
- support position sizing, trim/add decisions, and theme-level capital allocation

## Project Taxonomy
Every theme should use the same basket language:
- benchmark = broad comparison index or peer set
- core = highest-quality, most direct expressions of the thesis
- torque = higher-volatility or more asymmetric expressions
- canary = read-through names used to test whether the thesis is strengthening or weakening
- remove = names intentionally excluded because they no longer fit the thesis well

Do not use alternate category labels inside individual themes. Consistency matters more than creativity.

## What Counts as a Thesis
A thesis should be:
- economically meaningful
- specific enough to test over the next 4 to 6 quarters
- broad enough to have multiple public-equity expressions
- falsifiable
- tied to observable signals rather than vague narrative claims

A thesis is not usable unless it names:
- what should happen
- over what time frame
- what evidence would confirm it
- what evidence would disconfirm it
- which names best express it
- which names are better treated as canaries than holdings

Examples:
- AI infrastructure buildout is durable because power, cooling, networking, and optical bottlenecks remain binding even if model leadership rotates.
- SaaS shakeout is real but selective because workflow owners and platforms retain pricing power while weaker point solutions face consolidation pressure.

## Research Standards
Prefer the following source hierarchy:
1. SEC filings and official company reports
2. earnings releases, transcripts, and prepared remarks
3. management guidance and supplemental materials
4. reputable financial press for context
5. secondary commentary only when necessary

## Review Structure
Every thesis review should answer:
- What changed?
- What still holds?
- What is weakening?
- What is the strongest counter-narrative?
- What evidence would prove the thesis wrong?
- Which tickers are the best current expressions of the theme?
- Which tickers are canaries rather than holdings?
- What is the current theme health score?
- What action, if any, follows from that score?

## Review Cadence
- Monthly: theme-level review and health score update
- Quarterly: earnings and filings review for basket names
- Event-driven: major guide-downs, guide-ups, regulatory changes, or thesis-relevant news

## Time Horizon
Default time horizon is medium- to long-term:
- quarterly and annual fundamentals matter most
- this is not a short-term trading system
- volatility is acceptable if the thesis remains intact

## Primary Goal
Build a repeatable process for better thinking, better sizing, and better learning.
